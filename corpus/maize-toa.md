# Maize Production in Pakistan | Best Hybrid Varieties, Sowing Time & Yield Practices

By[Times of Agriculture](https://www.timesofagriculture.pk)



## 1. Maize Production Overview

**Maize Production in Pakistan** has grown from a minor cereal into one of the most performance-driven crops in the country. Farmers who once planted maize casually now treat it like a precision crop — and they’re right to do so. Maize reacts instantly to good management and punishes sloppy decisions. When seed, sowing time, plant population, nutrition, irrigation, and pest control are in sync, maize becomes one of the most reliable and profitable crops in Pakistan’s farming system.

Here’s the truth most people avoid saying openly: maize doesn’t forgive mistakes. If you sow late, yields collapse. If you ignore micronutrients, the ears stay small. If irrigation misses critical stages, kernel abortion shoots up. And if pest control is reactive instead of planned, you lose grain before you even see the damage. But when you get these factors right, maize transforms — it produces uniform cobs, substantial grain weight, and predictable returns.

#### 1.1 Why Maize Matters More Now

Maize is expanding faster than most other major crops for a few reasons:

* **Poultry & dairy demand:** Pakistan’s feed industry consumes a significant share of the national maize output.
* **Flexible rotation options:** It fits between wheat, cotton, rice, and fodder.
* **Hybrid technology:** Modern hybrids consistently outperform older OPVs by wide margins.
* **Short duration:** Early-maturing hybrids finish in 100–110 days, giving farmers cropping flexibility.
* **High-yield ceiling:** With proper management, maize beats wheat, cotton, and rice in per-acre profitability.

#### 1.2 Where Maize Is Expanding

Maize area is rising in:

* Central Punjab (Okara, Pakpattan, Sahiwal, Kasur)
* Northern Punjab (Chakwal, Jhelum, Attock)
* KP (Swat, Mardan, Charsadda, Swabi)
* Sindh under drip & pivot systems (Tando Allahyar, Mirpurkhas)
* Balochistan pockets (Khuzdar, Mastung)

**Farmers shift to maize where:**

* Water is limited
* Profit margins in cotton decline
* Land is available for short-rotation crops
* They want consistent returns

#### 1.3 What Limits Maize Yield in Pakistan

Despite strong potential, most fields fall short because of:

* Weak hybrid selection
* Late sowing
* Low plant population
* Nutrient imbalance (especially N, Zn, B, S)
* Poor irrigation scheduling
* Fall armyworm, stem borer, and early aphid infestations
* Late harvesting and poor drying

The problem isn’t the crop — it’s the lack of consistency in management.

#### 1.4 Why Maize Needs a Systems Approach

* Maize yield depends on **sequence**, not isolated actions.
* You can’t fix sowing errors with fertilizer.
* You can’t fix nutrient gaps with late irrigation.
* You can’t fix pest pressure with random spraying.

**Strong maize production is the result of:**

* Right hybrid
* Right planting time
* Right population
* Right fertility
* Right irrigation
* Right protection
* Right harvest timing

Put these together, and **Maize Production in Pakistan** becomes stable, profitable, and predictable — something farmers can bank on.

## 2. Importance of Maize in Pakistan

**Maize Production in Pakistan** is far more critical to the country’s food and feed system than most people realize. It isn’t just a cereal crop sitting behind wheat and rice in popularity — maize is the backbone of Pakistan’s poultry, cattle feed, and starch industries. And as demand for meat, milk, and processed foods rises, the importance of maize increases with it.

Here’s the thing: no other major crop in Pakistan has expanded as fast as maize over the last decade. Farmers are switching to maize because it grows quickly, yields reliably, and fits into multiple rotations without disrupting the system. Scientists value maize because it responds clearly to genetic improvements and agronomic decisions. And feed mills rely on it because maize delivers consistent energy, digestibility, and grain quality that animals convert efficiently.

#### 2.1 Economic Importance

Maize drives multiple high-value sectors, making it one of the most economically influential crops in Pakistan.

##### Key economic roles:

* Core ingredient for **poultry feed** (up to 55–60% of feed formulation)

When maize production stays stable, feed prices stabilize — and that steadies the poultry and dairy supply chains.

* Strong performance even in moderately dry areas

For many growers in Punjab and KP, maize is now a “bridge crop” that maintains cash flow between major seasons.

#### 2.3 Industrial Importance

Maize feeds Pakistan’s most significant industrial segments:

#### 3. Starch and ethanol industry

Maize plays a role in almost every major industrial value chain.

#### 2.4 Scientific Importance

Agricultural scientists track maize closely because it is highly responsive to:

* Nitrogen strategies
* Plant density
* Hybrid traits
* Irrigation timing
* Stress resistance
* Fall armyworm dynamics
* Precision agriculture techniques

Research on maize often serves as a baseline for other crops due to its clear physiological responses.

#### 2.5 Why Maize Will Become Even More Important

Demand for maize will continue to rise because:

* The poultry sector is expanding
* The dairy industry is growing
* Food processing is increasing
* Short-duration hybrids fit modern rotations
* Hybrid seed technology keeps improving
* Export potential is increasing

This makes **Maize Production in Pakistan** a central pillar of future agriculture planning.

## 3. Maize Growing Zones & Climate Requirements

**Maize Production in Pakistan** is susceptible to climate timing and regional conditions. Maize can deliver exceptional yields, but only when temperature, moisture, and sowing windows align. Unlike wheat, which tolerates a broader climatic range, maize reacts sharply to heat spikes, planting delays, and uneven moisture during grain formation.

Farmers who understand their agro-ecological zone — and match hybrid choice and sowing time accordingly — consistently outperform district averages.

#### 3.1 Major Maize Zones in Pakistan

Pakistan has distinct maize-producing regions, each with its own climatic strengths and challenges.

##### Punjab (Core Maize Belt)

* **Districts:** Okara, Pakpattan, Sahiwal, Kasur, Faisalabad, Sheikhupura
* **Climate:** Warm, ideal for spring and autumn maize
* **Strength:** High adoption of hybrid technology
* **Challenge:** Heat during silking if sowing is delayed

##### Northern Punjab (Rainfed / Barani Areas)

* **Districts:** Chakwal, Attock, Jhelum, Rawalpindi
* **Climate:** Monsoon-dependent, moderate summer
* **Strength:** Favorable for Kharif maize
* **Challenge:** Moisture stress and uneven rainfall

##### Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (Traditional Maize Zone)

* **Districts:** Swat, Mardan, Charsadda, Swabi
* **Climate:** Cool temperate zones
* **Strength:** Ideal for summer maize
* **Challenge:** Pest pressure is increasing due to climate shift

##### Sindh (Emerging Hybrid Zone)

* **Districts:** Tando Allahyar, Mirpurkhas, Badin
* **Climate:** Warm, long growing window
* **Strength:** Excellent for spring and drip-irrigated maize
* **Challenge:** High evaporative demand and water stress

##### Balochistan (Irrigated Pockets)

* **Districts:** Khuzdar, Mastung, Pishin
* **Climate:** Dry and warm
* **Strength:** Off-season growing potential under irrigation
* **Challenge:** Moisture and micronutrient deficiency

#### 3.2 Ideal Climate Conditions for Maize

Maize performs best when:

* **Day temperature:** 26–32°C
* **Night temperature:** 18–22°C
* **Humidity:** Moderate (not high)
* **Rainfall:** Well-distributed or irrigated
* **Sunlight:** Strong during vegetative growth and grain filling

##### Sensitive climatic periods:

* **Silking and tasseling:** Extremely sensitive to heat
* **Pollination:** Needs adequate moisture
* **Grain filling:** Needs stable irrigation

A single week of moisture stress during pollination can reduce yield by **15–25%**.

#### 3.3 Climate Risks Farmers Must Watch

##### Heat Stress

* Occurs with late sowing
* Reduces pollen viability
* Causes a poor kernel set

##### Cold Stress (Early Spring)

* Delays emergence
* Weakens seedling vigor

### **Drought Stress**

* Common in barani areas
* Leads to kernel abortion

##### Excess Rain / Humidity

* Increases fungal diseases
* Weakens grain quality

Matching sowing time with the climate is more critical than hybrid selection.

#### 3.4 Why Climate Matching Improves Yield

Strong alignment between climate and crop stage leads to:

* High pollen viability
* Strong cob formation
* Better silking
* Higher kernel rows
* More grain fill
* Lower pest pressure

All these factors combined make climate planning a core part of **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

## 5. Soil Requirements for Maize Crop

**Maize Production in Pakistan** depends heavily on soil conditions because maize is a deep-rooted, high-demand crop with fast early growth. It extracts large quantities of nutrients from the soil and needs a structure that supports rapid root expansion. When soil conditions are right—loose, fertile, well-drained, and rich in organic matter—maize plants develop robust root systems that support strong cob formation and uniform grain fill. When soil is compacted, nutrient-poor, or poorly drained, maize stays stunted and never recovers.

Here’s the truth: in maize, soil preparation is yield preparation. Good soil structure is a silent yield booster.

#### 5.1 Ideal Soil Types for Maize

Maize performs best in soils that provide adequate aeration and sufficient moisture.

##### Best soils for maize:

* **Sandy loam** — excellent drainage and root penetration
* **Loam** — ideal balance of moisture and aeration
* **Silty loam** — strong nutrient-holding ability
* **Clay loam** (if well-drained) — high fertility potential

##### Soils where maize struggles:

* Heavy clays that stay waterlogged
* Coarse sands with weak nutrient retention
* Hardpan soils restrict root depth
* Salt-affected soils (EC > 4 dS/m)

Even the best hybrid can’t perform in poorly structured soil.

#### 5.2 Soil Structure Requirements

Maize roots grow aggressively when the soil is:

* **Well aerated**
* **Deep and loose** (up to 4+ feet)
* **Free of compaction**
* **Moderately moist**
* **High in microbial activity**

**Vigorous root growth ensures:**

* Better nitrogen use
* More uniform cob size
* Improved drought resistance
* Faster recovery from early stress

#### 5.3 Soil Fertility Requirements

Maize is a nutrient-hungry crop. High yields depend on sufficient nutrient availability, particularly nitrogen.

##### Key fertility properties:

* Medium to high organic matter
* Balanced NPK
* Adequate sulfur
* Micronutrient availability (Zn, B)

##### Nutrient extraction (per acre):

* **Nitrogen:** Extremely high requirement
* **Phosphorus:** Important for root and cob formation
* **Potassium:** Critical for grain filling
* **Zinc:** Vital for seedling vigor and leaf development
* **Boron:** Important for pollen viability and cob setting

#### 5.4 Soil pH Requirements

Maize performs best in:

##### pH 6.0 – 7.5

Problems arise when pH is above 8.0:

* Micronutrients become unavailable
* Zinc deficiency becomes severe
* Phosphorus fixation increases
* Weak root development

Correcting pH improves fertilizer efficiency dramatically.

#### 5.5 Soil Preparation Steps for High Yield

##### Best practices:

* Deep plowing (once) to break compaction
* 2–3 cultivations to prepare a fine seedbed
* Laser leveling for uniform moisture
* Residue incorporation or removal (depending on crop rotation)
* Raised beds for heavy soils
* Ridge planting in flood-prone zones

##### Why soil preparation matters:

* Ensures uniform germination
* Prevents seedling stress
* Supports strong root penetration
* Improves plant uniformity

Uniform plants → uniform cobs → higher total yield.

#### 5.6 Organic Matter and Soil Health

Organic matter is a game changer in **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

##### Benefits of organic matter:

* Improves soil structure
* Enhances water infiltration
* Boosts microbial activity
* Reduces fertilizer loss
* Improves nutrient retention

**Sources include:**

* FYM (Farmyard manure)
* Press mud
* Compost
* Poultry manure
* Green manure crops (sunhemp, guar)

#### 5.7 Salt-Affected Soils and Maize

If EC exceeds **4 dS/m**, maize performance drops significantly.

##### Management solutions:

* Gypsum application
* Organic matter addition
* Improved drainage
* Salt-tolerant hybrids (where available)

#### 5.8 Why Soil Quality Directly Controls Maize Yield

Healthy soil translates into:

* Better plant height
* Larger leaves
* Stronger ear development
* Higher kernel rows per cob
* More uniform grain fill

Soil is not a background factor — it is the root of all strong **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

## **6. Maize La**n**d Preparation Techniques**

**Maize Production in Pakistan** depends heavily on how well the land is prepared before sowing. Maize is exceptionally responsive to seedbed quality because early root establishment determines plant vigor, cob size, and uniformity. A well-prepared field ensures rapid germination, balanced moisture, strong early growth, and smooth nutrient uptake. A poorly prepared field leads to uneven emergence, stunted early development, and reduced grain yield — and the crop never fully recovers.

Here’s what most farmers get wrong: they treat maize like wheat when preparing land, but maize needs deeper, cleaner, and more structured soil for high-performance rooting.

#### 6.1 Objectives of Proper Land Preparation

A good maize seedbed must:

* Provide **fine, crumbly soil** for uniform seed placement
* Remove weeds and crop residues that compete early
* Break compacted layers to support deep rooting
* Improve water infiltration
* Ensure uniform moisture during germination
* Allow machinery to operate smoothly

The goal is simple: **maximum germination and maximum early vigor.**

#### 6.2 Land Preparation Steps for High-Yield Maize

##### Step 1: Residue Management

* Remove heavy residues from previous crops, such as sugarcane or rice.
* Incorporate wheat residues using a rotavator or disk plough.
* Avoid burning — it reduces soil organic matter and hurts yield.

### **Step 2: Deep Ploughing**

* Use a moldboard plough or chisel plough once.
* Break hardpan layers to a depth of 10–12 inches.
* Improves root penetration and moisture infiltration.

##### Step 3: Cultivation and Harrowing

* 2–3 cultivations with harrowing in between.
* Break soil clods and create a fine tilth.
* Helps uniform germination and reduces patchy emergence.

##### Step 4: Laser Leveling

* Essential for maize, especially under flood irrigation.
* Ensures equal water distribution.
* Prevents water stress during early growth.
* Saves 20–25% irrigation water.

##### Step 5: Pre-Sowing Irrigation (Rouni)

* Irrigate 7–10 days before sowing.
* Allows weeds to germinate—the stale seedbed technique.
* Kill these weeds before planting to reduce early competition.

##### Step 6: Final Seedbed Preparation

* Light cultivation + planking before the seed drill.
* Ensures a firm, smooth surface for accurate seed placement.

#### 6.3 Land Preparation for Different Soil Types

##### Sandy soils:

* Focus on moisture conservation
* Use compost and organic matter
* Avoid deep ploughing

##### Loam soils:

* Ideal structure; standard preparation is sufficient
* Ensure good leveling

##### Clay loam soils:

* Use raised beds or ridges
* Avoid waterlogging
* Deep ploughing is helpful

##### Heavy clay soils:

* Not ideal for maize
* A deep ripper may be needed
* Avoid sowing immediately after rain

#### 6.4 Bed and Ridge Planting Benefits

Raised bed and ridge methods outperform flat sowing under high rainfall or heavy soil conditions.

##### Advantages:

* Better drainage
* Stronger root health
* Reduced disease pressure
* Less lodging
* Higher oxygen availability

These methods are standard in heavy irrigation districts of Punjab and Sindh.

#### 6.5 Zero Tillage Maize

Zero till maize is emerging in residue-based systems.

##### Works best after:

* Wheat
* Rice (with proper residue management)

##### Benefits:

* Saves fuel and labor
* Conserves soil moisture
* Reduces erosion
* Supports soil health

Zero till is ideal in irrigated wheat–maize systems where soil structure is already established.

#### 6.6 Why Land Preparation Affects Maize Yield

Good land prep ensures:

* Uniform emergence (critical for cob uniformity)
* Strong root anchorage
* Balanced moisture during early growth
* Better nutrient uptake
* Lower weed pressure
* Higher crop stand

Proper land preparation alone can increase yield by **10–15%** without adding any extra fertilizer.

#### 6.7 Summary: The Success Formula for Maize Land Preparation

Your maize field is ready when it is:

* Deeply loosened
* Weed-free
* Moist but not waterlogged
* Level and firm
* Fine-tilled
* Suitable for uniform seed placement

Strong land preparation sets the foundation for successful **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

## 7. Ideal Maize Sowing Time in Pakistan

**Maize Production in Pakistan** depends more on sowing time than almost any other factor. Even the best hybrid fails if planted outside its suitable climate window. Maize is susceptible to temperature during germination, vegetative growth, tasseling, silking, and grain filling. A delay of even 10–12 days can expose the crop to heat stress, pollen failure, poor kernel set, and higher pest pressure.

The farmers who consistently achieve top yields are those who sow *on time*, not those who sow *more acres* or *apply more fertilizer.*

#### 7.1 Spring Sowing Time (Punjab & Sindh)

Spring maize benefits from cool early temperatures and warm conditions during grain fill.

##### Punjab (Spring Season):

* **Optimal window:15 January – 15 February**
* **Extended window:** till **25 February**

##### Sindh (Spring Season):

* **Optimal window January – 10 February**

##### Why these windows work:

* Avoid cold stress during germination
* Escape heat stress during silking
* Better pollen viability
* Firm grain filling before the hot months

Late sowing exposes maize to heat during pollination, causing **kernel abortion**.

#### 7.2 Autumn Sowing Time (Punjab & KP)

Autumn maize fits after wheat harvest and performs well if sown early.

##### Punjab (Autumn Season):

* **Optimal window:25 July – 20 August**
* **Latest safe window:25 August**

##### KP (Summer Maize):

* **Optimal window:10 June – 20 July**

##### Why timing matters:

* Ensures tasseling during moderate temperatures
* Prevents moisture stress during grain fill
* Avoids late-season armyworm pressure

Late autumn sowing is affected by shorter day length and poor light for cob formation.

#### 7.3 Kharif (Rainfed) Maize Sowing Time

#### Northern Punjab & Barani Areas:

* **Optimal window: 15 June – 15 July**

Rainfed maize depends entirely on rainfall timing.

**Timely sowing ensures:**

* Better root establishment
* Stronger vegetative growth
* Reduced risk of drought stress
* Better pollination under moderate temperatures

#### 7.4 Off-Season / Winter Maize Under Tunnels

Certain areas in Punjab (Sheikhupura, Kasur) grow off-season maize.

##### Planting window:

* **Mid-September – Early October**
* Protected with low tunnels or plastic

##### Best uses:

* Silage
* Fresh green maize markets
* Early feed production

Off-season maize needs special care due to cold sensitivity.

#### 7.5 Temperature Requirements at Sowing

For strong germination, soil temperature should be:

* **Minimum:** 10–12°C
* **Optimal:** 16–20°C

**Sowing too early causes:**

* Slow germination
* Patchy emergence
* Weak seedlings

**Sowing too late causes:**

* Heat damage during silking
* Poor kernel set
* Reduced grain weight

#### 7.6 Consequences of Late Sowing

Late planting leads to:

* Heat stress during pollination
* Low pollen viability
* Reduced kernel rows
* Poor grain depth
* Increased pest pressure (especially armyworm)
* Lower overall yield

Late sowing is one of the top reasons for yield loss in **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

#### 7.7 How to Ensure Timely Sowing on Every Farm

* Prepare the land early
* Book a seat in advance
* Monitor local temperature trends
* Schedule irrigation for seedbed moisture
* Use laser leveling to speed up operations
* Reduce delays between crops

Timely sowing is a habit — not a lucky coincidence.

#### 7.8 Summary: The Golden Rule of Maize Sowing

**Right hybrid + right sowing time = stable high yield.**

You can’t compensate for wrong timing with fertilizer, irrigation, or pesticides. The biological window of maize is fixed — respect it, and the crop rewards you.

## 8. Recommended Seed Rate and Plant Population

**Maize Production in Pakistan** reaches its full potential only when the plant population is correct and uniform. Maize is susceptible to spacing — too few plants reduce total cob count and yield; too many plants cause competition, weak stems, small ears, and poor grain filling. Getting the seed rate right is one of the easiest yield boosters farmers often ignore.

Here’s the reality: a perfect maize field looks like a factory — every plant the same height, same girth, same cob position, same grain maturity. That uniformity begins with correct plant population.

#### 8.1 Ideal Plant Population for Grain Maize

The best-performing plant population for Pakistani conditions is:

##### Recommended range:

**26,000 – 32,000 plants per acre**

This range gives:

* Strong root development
* Uniform plant height
* Balanced sunlight capture
* Maximum cob size
* Higher grain per ear

Below 24,000 plants/acre, yield drops sharply.

Above 34,000 plants/acre, competition reduces ear size.

#### 8.2 Seed Rate for Hybrid Maize

Hybrid maize has high seed germination, so you don’t need heavy seed rates.

##### Seed rate:

**8–10 kg per acre** (depending on seed size)

##### Factors affecting seed rate:

* Seed size (small seeds require less kg)
* Germination percentage
* Soil type
* Planting method (drill, bed, ridge)

#### 8.3 Row Spacing and Plant Spacing

Spacing decides cob uniformity and photosynthesis efficiency.

##### Row spacing:

* **30 inches** (standard and effective)
* **20 inches** under high-density modern systems
* **25 inches** in fertile irrigated zones

##### Plant-to-plant spacing:

* **6–8 inches** for regular hybrids
* **8–9 inches** for large cob hybrids
* **5–6 inches** for early maturing hybrids

Correct spacing increases light interception — boosting grain fill.

#### 8.4 Plant Population for Silage Maize

For silage growers:

##### Silage population:

**40,000 – 45,000 plants per acre**

##### Why a higher population?

* More biomass
* Thinner stems
* Higher moisture content
* Faster canopy closure

Silage needs mass, not grain size.

#### 8.5 Plant Population for Rainfed (Barani) Maize

##### Recommended:

**18,000 – 22,000 plants per acre**

Lower population reduces drought stress and improves Survival.

#### 8.6 Importance of Uniform Emergence

Maize punishes uneven emergence because late-emerging plants:

* Become shaded
* Produce smaller cobs
* Have fewer kernels
* Steal nutrients without contributing much yield

##### Emergence should be:

* **95%+** germination
* All plants emerging within **48 hours**

Delayed seedlings reduce total yield more than missing plants.

#### 8.7 Factors That Reduce Germination

* Old seed
* Poor soil moisture
* Deep sowing (>2 inches)
* Cold soil
* Hardpan layer
* Poor seed-soil contact
* Weedy seedbeds
* Insect damage (wireworms, termites)

Fixing seedbed quality often boosts stand establishment by 10–15%.

#### 8.8 How to Ensure Correct Population

* Use a seed drill calibrated before planting
* Maintain even seed depth (1.5–2 inches)
* Ensure proper soil moisture
* Avoid cloddy fields
* Conduct germination tests on seed lots
* Monitor early crops daily

A substantial plant population is one of the most controllable yield components in **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

#### 8.9 Summary: Population = Profit

A higher population does not mean a higher yield.

Correct population means higher yield.

Uniformity creates stable results, especially under stress.

## 9. Maize Fertilizer Schedule & Nutrient Management

**Maize Production in Pakistan** is only as strong as its nutrient program. Maize pulls large amounts of nutrients from the soil — far more than wheat, rice, or cotton — because it grows quickly, produces heavy biomass, and fills dense kernels that demand a constant nutrient supply. If nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, sulfur, zinc, or boron fall short at the wrong stage, the crop shows it immediately through pale leaves, weak cobs, poor kernel rows, and stunted grain filling.

Here’s the thing: most maize fields don’t fail because farmers apply *too little fertilizer* — they fail because fertilizer is used *at the wrong time*. Timing is far more critical than total quantity.

#### 9.1 Baseline Fertilizer Requirement (Per acre)

This is the practical, field-tested nutrient requirement for commercial hybrids:

#### Total Nutrient Requirement:

* **Nitrogen (N): 120–160 kg/acre**
* **Phosphorus (P2O5): 60–80 kg/acre**
* **Potassium (K2O): 40–60 kg/acre**
* **Sulfur (S): 10–20 kg/acre**
* **Zinc (Zn): 5–10 kg/acre**
* **Boron (B): 1–2 kg/acre**

Hybrid maize will NOT show full potential if even one of these nutrients is missing.

#### 9.2 Fertilizer Application Schedule (Time-Based)

#### **1. At Sowing** (Very Critical)

##### Apply:

* **All phosphorus** (DAP or SSP)
* **All sulfur**
* **Half potassium**
* **10–20% nitrogen**

##### Why?

These nutrients drive root growth, early vigor, and rapid leaf expansion.

#### **2. At 25–30 Days (V6–V8 Stage)**

##### Apply:

* **40% nitrogen**
* **Remaining potassium**

This stage determines plant height, leaf size, and cob initiation.

#### 3. At 45–55 Days (Pre-Tasseling)

##### Apply:

* 40–50% nitrogen

This is the most essential nitrogen stage — it directly affects cob length, grain rows, and pollen strength.

#### 4. At Silking (Optional, if crop is strong)

##### Apply micronutrients:

* **Zinc foliar spray**
* **Boron spray**

These boost pollination and grain formation.

#### 9.3 Best Fertilizer Sources

* Urea
* CAN (better under heat)
* NP blends (for starter dose)

##### Phosphorus sources

* DAP (best overall)

##### Sulfur sources

If you see these symptoms, your yield is already dropping.

#### 9.5 Foliar Nutrition (Powerful Under Stress)

Foliar feeding is highly effective during heat or water stress.

##### Recommended foliar sprays:

#### 9.6 Role of Organic Matter in Maize Nutrition

#### 9.7 Why Wrong Nutrient Timing Destroys Yield

* Nitrogen loss
* Weak cob formation

Your nitrogen belongs WITH the crop — not before it.

Correct timing is the real yield-booster in **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

## 10. Irrigation Requirements for Maize Crop

**Maize Production in Pakistan** depends critically on precise irrigation timing. Maize doesn’t need as much water as rice, but it does require water at specific, biologically sensitive stages. A single missed irrigation during tasseling or grain filling can drop yield by 20–40%. The truth is simple: maize isn’t a “water-thirsty crop” — it’s a “timing-sensitive crop.”

Here’s what separates high-yield maize farmers from average ones:

They never irrigate “by schedule.” They irrigate by **crop stage**.

#### 10.1 Total Irrigation Requirement (Per acre)

Under normal conditions, maize needs:

##### Total irrigations: 4–7

Depending on soil type and season.

##### Breakdown by region:

* **Punjab (spring):** 5–6 irrigations
* **Punjab (autumn):** 4–5 irrigations
* **KP (summer):** 5–7 irrigations
* **Sindh:** 6–7 irrigations
* **Rainfed (barani):** only rainfall + supplemental irrigation

##### 10.2 Critical Stages for Irrigation

Certain stages absolutely cannot be missed.

##### 1. Germination & Early Growth (0–15 days)

* Essential for strong root establishment
* Moist but not soggy soil

##### 2. Knee-High Stage (20–30 days)

* Supports rapid leaf growth
* Prepares crop for cob initiation

##### 3. Pre-Tasseling / Tasseling (45–60 days)

**Most critical irrigation of the entire crop.**

* Supports pollen formation
* Prevents tassel blasting
* Supports silk growth

##### 4. Silking Stage (55–70 days)

**The second most crucial irrigation.**

* Low moisture causes poor pollination
* Leads to a reduced kernel set

##### 5. Grain Filling (70–95 days)

* Water shortage = kernel abortion
* Affects grain depth and test weight

##### 6. Dough Stage (optional but beneficial)

* Helps complete grain formation
* Improves grain weight

Missing irrigation at silking can destroy even the best hybrid.

#### **10.3 Irrigation Intervals by Soil Type**

##### Sandy soils:

* Irrigate every **5–6 days**
* High infiltration but low retention

##### Loam soils:

* Irrigate every **7–10 days**
* Best balance

##### Clay loam soils:

* Irrigate every **10–14 days**
* Retains moisture longer

Waterlogging kills maize roots — avoid it at all costs.

#### 10.4 Signs of Water Stress in Maize

Watch for these visual indicators:

* Leaf rolling (morning or midday)
* Blue-green leaf color
* Drooping leaves
* Poor silk emergence
* Slow tassel growth
* Uneven cob development

Once stress appears, yield loss has already started.

##### 10.5 Modern Irrigation Methods for Maize

##### Drip irrigation:

* Saves 40–50% water
* Uniform moisture
* Works well in sandy soils
* Supports fertigation

##### Sprinkler irrigation (pivot systems):

* Very effective in Sindh and Balochistan
* Strong for large-scale operations
* Reduces labor costs

##### Furrow irrigation:

* Common in Punjab
* Works well with beds/ridges

##### Flood irrigation:

* Least efficient
* Causes waterlogging
* Should be replaced with bed/furrow

## **10.6 Best Irrigation Practices for Maximum Yield**

* Laser leveling before sowing
* Avoid irrigation 3–4 days before sowing
* Irrigate lightly at germination
* Ensure full moisture at tasseling
* Avoid deep irrigation during the early stages
* Maintain soil moisture at silking
* Stop irrigation at physiological maturity

#### 10.7 Irrigation Mistakes Farmers Must Avoid

* Over-irrigation early → weak roots
* Waterlogging → root rot
* Irrigating during midday heat
* Skipping irrigation at tasseling
* Flooding heavy soils
* Using canal water with high salinity

A single irrigation mistake can undo weeks of good management.

#### 10.8 Why Irrigation Timing Controls Yield

When irrigation matches crop demand:

* Root health improves
* Cob size increases
* Pollination succeeds
* Kernel rows increase
* Kernel depth improves
* Grain filling accelerates

In short, irrigation timing drives the success of **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

#### 11. Major Maize Pests and IPM Strategies

**Maize Production in Pakistan** has become increasingly vulnerable to pests, especially after the spread of fall armyworm (FAW). Maize is a high-energy crop, rich in sugars and proteins, which makes it an attractive target for a wide range of insects. Poor scouting, late sprays, and overuse of broad-spectrum chemicals create resistance and wipe out beneficial insects — the exact opposite of what a productive maize field needs.

Here’s the blunt truth: most maize yield losses in Pakistan come from **late detection**, not from the pests themselves.

#### 11.1 Major Insect Pests of Maize in Pakistan

##### 1. Fall Armyworm (FAW)

The most damaging maize pest today.

##### Damage:

* Skeletonizes leaves
* Attacks funnel stage
* Bores into cob
* Reduces yield by 20–50% if uncontrolled

##### Signs:

* Window-paning on leaves
* Frass (sawdust-like material) in the funnel
* Holes in the cob husk

#### 2. Stem Borer (Chilo partellus)

Common in spring and autumn crops.

##### Damage:

* Deadheart in the early stage
* Tunneling inside the stem
* Weakening of the cob and grain development

#### 3. Termites

Damage seeds and early seedlings.

##### Damage:

* Patchy germination
* Weak plants
* Hollow stems

#### 4. Aphids

Early-season pest.

##### Damage:

* Sticky honeydew
* Virus transmission
* Weak seedlings

#### 5. Corn Earworm

Attacks at the grain filling stage.

##### Damage:

* Feeds on kernels
* Reduces grain weight
* Increases fungal infection

## 11.2 IPM (Integrated Pest Management) plan

IPM is the most innovative, cheapest, and most effective way to protect maize.

##### 1. Field Monitoring (Scouting)

Scout fields **twice a week**.

Look for:

* Eggs on leaf undersides
* Funnel feeding damage
* Leaf perforations
* Frass in whorl
* Silks chewed or cut

##### 2. Use of Pheromone Traps

Essential for early detection of FAW and stem borers.

* Install **10 traps per acre**
* Check twice weekly
* Helps time sprays accurately

##### 3. Biological Control

* *Trichogramma* egg parasitoids
* Neem-based formulations
* Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt formulations)

Safest for predators and pollinators.

#### 11.3 Threshold Levels for Spray Decisions

Spraying below threshold = wasted money

Spraying above threshold = delayed control

##### FAW Threshold

* If **5% plants** show damage at the early stage → spray immediately

##### Stem Borer Threshold

* **10% deadheart** or **one larva per plant**

##### Aphids Threshold

* Aphid colonies are visible on 20% plants.

Threshold-based spraying avoids resistance.

#### 11.4 Recommended Insecticides (Rotation-Based)

##### For Fall Armyworm

* Chlorantraniliprole (Coragen)
* Emamectin benzoate
* Spinetoram
* Lufenuron

##### For Stem Borer

* Lambda-cyhalothrin
* Cypermethrin
* Chlorpyrifos (avoid excessive use)

##### For Aphids

* Imidacloprid
* Thiamethoxam

##### Rotation rules:

* Never repeat the same Mode of action
* Avoid consecutive sprays of pyrethroids
* Mix only as recommended

#### 11.5 Cultural Practices for Pest Prevention

* Early sowing escapes peak pest cycles
* Deep ploughing destroys overwintering larvae
* Proper residue management
* Balanced nitrogen (avoid excess)
* Maintain a substantial plant population
* Keep field bunds weed-free

These measures reduce pest pressure by **30–40%** without the use of chemicals.

#### 11.6 Pest Control Mistakes Farmers Must Avoid

* Spraying without scouting
* Using tank mixes mindlessly
* Overusing pyrethroids
* Ignoring early funnel damage
* Late spraying (after larvae enter the cob)
* Applying high nitrogen without potassium

These mistakes lead to catastrophic yield loss.

#### 11.7 Why IPM Protects Yield More Than Spraying

IPM improves:

* Cob weight
* Grain uniformity
* Kernel rows
* Plant health
* Pollinator safety
* Resistance management

When IPM is applied correctly, **Maize Production in Pakistan** becomes more stable, predictable, and profitable.

## 12. Maize Diseases and Effective Control

**Maize Production in Pakistan** faces increasing disease pressure due to climate shifts, changes in humidity, continuous maize cultivation, and residue buildup. Unlike pests, maize diseases often remain unnoticed until they cause significant damage — and by the time symptoms appear, the disease has already reduced cob size, kernel depth, and grain weight. That’s why prevention, early identification, and timely control matter far more than curative spraying.

The truth is simple: disease-free maize fields produce stronger cobs, better test weight, and more uniform grain quality. Poor disease management silently kills yield.

#### 12.1 Major Diseases of Maize in Pakistan

#### 1. Turcicum Leaf Blight (TLB)

The most common disease in humid areas.

##### Symptoms:

* Long, cigar-shaped brown lesions
* Rapid spread in humid weather
* Premature death of leaves

##### Impact:

Reduces photosynthesis → reduces grain fill.

#### 2. Maydis Leaf Blight (MLB)

Common in hot and dry regions.

##### Symptoms:

* Small, oval brown spots
* Increase during warm, dry weather

##### **Impact**:

Weakens leaf area and lowers grain weight.

#### 3. Bacterial Leaf Streak

Appearing more frequently due to climate change.

##### Symptoms:

* Narrow water-soaked streaks
* Turns brown with yellow edges

##### Impact:

Weakens upper canopy → poor cob growth.

#### 4. Downy Mildew (DM)

Severe disease in KP and irrigated areas.

##### Symptoms:

* Yellow-white streaks
* Leaf shredding
* Stunted plants

##### Impact:

Destroys entire fields if unmanaged early.

#### 5. Rust (Common Rust & Southern Rust)

Favors cool, humid conditions.

##### Symptoms:

* Reddish-brown pustules
* Rapid spread in moist weather

##### **Impac**t**:**

Reduces leaf life → weak kernel set.

#### 6. Charcoal Rot

Common in sandy soils and drought years.

##### Symptoms:

* Stem becomes brittle
* Black fungal growth inside the stem

##### Impact:

Causes lodging and yield loss.

#### 7. Ear Rot (Fusarium, Aspergillus)

Occurs in wet or humid conditions.

##### Symptoms:

* Mold on kernels
* Off-colored grain
* Poor grain weight

##### Impact:

Reduces grain quality and marketability.

#### 12.2 Disease-Favorable Conditions

##### Diseases increase when:

* Humidity is high
* Residues remain on the soil
* Maize is grown year after year in the same field
* Nitrogen is excessive
* Potassium is deficient
* Crop canopy stays wet overnight

Preventing these conditions is half the control plan.

#### 12.3 Preventive Disease Management (Most Important)

##### 1. Resistant Hybrids

Choose hybrids with built-in resistance.

### **2. Crop Rotation**

Avoid maize after maize.

**Use rotation:**

* Wheat → Maize
* Legumes → Maize

##### 3. Seed Treatment

**Treat seed with:**

* Metalaxyl
* Thiram
* Captan

Prevents early fungal infections.

##### 4. Proper Plant Spacing

Improves airflow and reduces humidity.

##### 5. Balanced Fertilization

* Avoid overuse of nitrogen
* Supply adequate potassium
* Provide zinc and boron

Balanced nutrition increases disease resilience.

#### 12.4 Chemical Control (When Necessary)

Use fungicides only after confirming the disease.

##### Effective Fungicides for Maize Diseases:

* **Azoxystrobin**
* **Propiconazole**
* **Tebuconazole**
* **Mancozeb**

##### Application Timing:

* First spray: early symptoms
* Second spray: 10–12 days later

##### For Downy Mildew:\*\*

* Metalaxyl-based fungicides are essential.

#### 12.5 IPDM (Integrated Disease and Pest Management)

Combined pest + disease management gives the best results.

##### Key IPDM practices:

* Weekly field scouting
* Removing infected plants early
* Avoiding overhead irrigation
* Cleaning field borders
* Maintaining field hygiene
* Rotating fungicides

This reduces both pest and disease pressure.

#### 12.6 Mistakes Farmers Commonly Make

* Spraying too late
* Overloading nitrogen
* Skipping potash
* Ignoring the first symptoms
* Growing the same hybrid year after year
* Mixing fungicides incorrectly
* Applying fungicides in midday heat

Each one of these errors destroys cob development.

#### 12.7 Why Disease Management Protects Yield

Healthy plants maintain:

* Strong photosynthesis
* High canopy life
* Full grain filling
* Strong kernel depth
* Higher test weight

Disease-free maize fields produce consistent, market-grade grain — the foundation of sustainable **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

## 13. Weed Control Techniques in Maize Fields

**Maize Production in Pakistan** is heavily influenced by weed pressure, especially during the first 30–40 days after sowing. Maize grows slowly early on, while weeds grow aggressively and steal water, nutrients, sunlight, and space. If weeds dominate early, maize never fully recovers — even if you spray later or add more fertilizer. Early weed control is the cheapest and most effective way to protect yield.

Here’s the reality most farmers overlook: weed competition during the first month silently destroys cob size, kernel rows, and grain depth. Clean fields produce strong maize.

#### 13.1 Critical Weed-Free Period

The most critical time to keep maize weed-free is:

##### 0–40 days after sowing

If weed competition occurs during this period:

* Plant height decreases
* Cob initiation weakens
* Ear size reduces
* Kernel formation declines
* Grain filling becomes shallow

A weed-free first month can increase yield by **15–25%**.

#### 13.2 Major Weeds in Maize Fields (Pakistan)

##### Broadleaf weeds:

* Bathu (Chenopodium album)
* Itsit (Trianthema portulacastrum)
* Krund (Amaranthus viridis)
* Jangli Palak (Rumex dentatus)

##### Grassy weeds:

* Dumbi Sitti (Cynodon dactylon)
* Swanki (Echinochloa colona)
* Madana Grass (Setaria spp.)

##### Sedges:

* Purple nut sedge (Cyperus rotundus)

These weeds absorb nitrogen more quickly than maize, resulting in severe early competition.

#### 13.3 Weed Control Options

##### 1. Pre-Emergence Herbicides (Right After Sowing)

Apply within **48 hours** after sowing.

##### Effective pre-emergence herbicides:

* **Atrazine**
* **S-metolachlor**
* **Pendimethalin**
* **Mesotrione-based pre-mix**

##### Benefits:

* Stops early weed emergence
* Keeps the field clean for 20–25 days
* Reduces manual labor cost

#### 2. Early Post-Emergence Herbicides (15–25 Days)

Use once maize has 2–4 leaves.

##### Recommended products:

* **Mesotrione**
* **Nicosulfuron**
* **Topramezone**
* **Halosulfuron** (for sedges)

##### Benefits:

* Controls surviving weeds
* Protects cob initiation
* Works well under irrigated systems

##### 3. Manual & Mechanical Weeding

Especially effective in:

* Barani areas
* Small farms
* Ridge/beds planting systems

##### Advantages:

* Breaks soil crust
* Improves aeration
* Reduces disease humidity

##### 4. Herbicide Rotation

Essential to prevent resistance.

**Rotate between:**

* Triazines
* HPPD-inhibitors
* Sulfonylureas

Never rely on a single herbicide group.

#### 13.4 Cultural Practices to Reduce Weed Pressure

* Use clean seedbeds
* Laser leveling avoids wet patches
* Optimum seed rate improves canopy closure
* Balanced fertilization reduces weak patches
* Bed or ridge planting in heavy soils
* Timely first irrigation

A strong early crop outcompetes weeds naturally.

#### 13.5 Common Weed Control Mistakes

* Late herbicide application
* Spraying in dry soil
* Using the wrong dose
* Not rotating herbicides
* Allowing weeds to seed
* Using poor-quality spray nozzles
* Flooding fields after herbicide application

Each mistake increases weed pressure for the entire season.

#### 13.6 Why Early Weed Control Is Non-Negotiable

Clean early fields ensure:

* Strong vegetative growth
* Big cobs
* Deep kernel rows
* Higher grain weight
* Uniform grain moisture

Weed management is one of the simplest and highest-return decisions in **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

## 14. Maize Harvesting and Grain Quality

**Maize Production in Pakistan** reaches its full potential only when the crop is harvested at the right time and handled appropriately. Many farmers focus heavily on sowing, fertilization, and spraying — but then lose money at the final step due to premature or late harvesting, high-moisture grain, or poor post-harvest handling. Grain quality affects price, storage safety, feed value, and shelf life. If grain quality drops, all the hard work put in earlier goes to waste.

Here’s the reality: harvest timing decides whether your grain gets a Premium price or becomes low-grade feed.

#### 14.1 Ideal Harvesting Time

Maize should be harvested when:

* **Grain moisture:** 18–20%
* **Physiological maturity:** black layer visible at kernel base
* **Cob husk:** dry and papery
* **Stalks:** beginning to dry
* **Kernels:** firm and fully dented (in dent hybrids)

### **Why this timing works:**

* Ensures maximum grain weight
* Reduces kernel damage
* Minimizes fungal infections
* Improves test weight
* Reduces mechanical harvesting losses

Harvesting too early → low weight

Harvesting too late → fungal buildup + storage loss

#### 14.2 Indicators of Maize Maturity

Look for:

* Kernel changes from milky → dough → dent → black layer
* Husks turn brown
* The stalk becomes partially dry
* Grain moisture begins dropping naturally
* Kernels resist denting under pressure

A mature kernel has a visible **black layer** — a natural sign that grain filling is complete.

#### 14.3 Harvesting Methods

##### 1. Manual Harvesting

Still common in many regions.

##### Benefits:

* Less mechanical damage
* Suitable for smaller farms

##### Limitations:

* Labor intensive
* Slower
* Risk of late harvesting

#### 2. Mechanical Harvesting (Combine Harvesters)

Increasingly used in large maize-growing belts.

##### Benefits:

* Fast and efficient
* Lower labor cost
* Excellent for uniform hybrids

##### Limitations:

* Needs well-dried plants
* Possible grain cracking if the machine is not calibrated

#### 14.4 Post-Harvest Handling & Drying

This is where many farmers make costly mistakes.

##### Key steps:

1. **Dry grain to 12–14% moisture**
   1. Prevents fungal growth
   1. Makes storage safe
2. **Use solar or forced-air drying**
3. **Avoid drying on bare soil**
4. **Store grain in clean, moisture-free rooms**
5. **Prevent contact with rodents and insects**

##### Why moisture matters:

Moist grain supports mold growth, heats up in storage, and loses market value rapidly.

#### 14.5 Grain Quality Factors That Traders and Feed Mills Check

To get Premium rates, grain must be:

* **Clean** (low dust, low trash)
* **Uniform** (consistent kernel size)
* **Physiologically mature**
* **Free from fungus and insects**
* **Low moisture (≤14%)**
* **High test weight**
* **Bright kernel color**

Better grain quality earns better prices in both feed and processing industries.

#### 14.6 Common Harvesting and Post-Harvest Mistakes

* Harvesting too early (high moisture)
* Delaying harvest after maturity
* Leaving harvested cobs in the field overnight
* Drying grain on the soil
* Storing grain in humid stacks
* Using contaminated storage bags
* Mixing healthy and moldy grain

Each of these mistakes reduces grain quality and increases storage loss.

##### 14.7 Improving Grain Quality Through Better Practices

To maximize grain quality:

* Harvest at physiological maturity
* Dry grain quickly and evenly
* Use proper aeration
* Avoid kernel cracking
* Keep storage areas cool and dry
* Maintain grain moisture below 14%

Good harvest handling can increase the sale price by 8–12%.

#### 14.8 Why Grain Quality Matters for Pakistan’s Maize Market

High-quality maize results in:

* Higher feed conversion efficiency
* Better starch extraction
* Stronger feed industry demand
* Lower storage toxicity risk
* Improved export potential

A strong harvest program is the final step in high-value **Maize Production in Pakistan**.

## FAQ’s

Spring: **15 January–15 February (Punjab)**; Autumn: **25 July–20 August (Punjab)**; Sindh spring: **1 January–10 February** — sowing inside these windows avoids heat at silking and reduces kernel abortion.

Top performers include **DKC series (DKC 9108/9130), Pioneer 30T60/31R88, Syngenta NK lines, and local high-yield hybrids** — choose based on district climate, sowing window, and end use (grain vs. silage).

Use **8–10 kg/acre seed** to achieve **26,000–32,000 plants/acre** for grain maize; adjust for germination and aim for >95% uniform emergence.

Apply full P and S at sowing; split N into **three doses** (starter 10–20%, 40% at V6–V8, and remaining 40–50% pre-tassel). Nitrogen before tassel is the single most yield-critical N application.

Scout twice weekly, use pheromone traps, apply biologicals (Bt) for early larvae, spray selective insecticides only at thresholds (>5% plants damaged for FAW), and rotate modes of action to prevent resistance.
