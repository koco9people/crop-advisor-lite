# Cotton Production in Pakistan | Varieties, Pest Management & Yield Optimization

By[Times of Agriculture](https://www.timesofagriculture.pk)



## 1. Cotton Production Overview

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** remains the backbone of the country’s agricultural economy, no matter how much cropping patterns shift. The crop feeds a massive industrial chain that includes ginning units, textile factories, oil extraction units, feed mills, exporters, and thousands of small rural enterprises. But here’s the truth: Cotton today isn’t performing at its full potential—not because farmers don’t work hard, but because the crop demands precision in areas where small mistakes cost big yields.

Cotton is a long-duration plant that responds to every decision across its growth cycle. Sowing too early or too late changes pest pressure. Weak soil preparation restricts root spread. Poor irrigation timing causes square shedding and boll drop. Nutrient imbalance affects fibre length and micronaire. When farmers align these factors well, Cotton becomes one of the strongest cash crops in the system; when they miss the timing, no amount of spraying or fertilizer will make up for it.

In research trials across Punjab and Sindh, the most significant performance gaps aren’t due to climate alone—they’re due to management differences. Fields that maintain balanced nitrogen, avoid over-irrigation, manage whitefly pressure early, and prevent pink bollworm buildup consistently outperform neighbouring farms planted with the same variety. This reinforces what scientists and experienced growers already know: **Cotton Production in Pakistan** is a management-driven crop, not a luck-driven one.

## 2. Importance of Cotton in Pakistan

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** carries an economic influence that goes far beyond the farm. Cotton isn’t just another Kharif crop; it’s tied to national income, industrial jobs, export stability, and the livelihood of millions. When Cotton performs well, multiple sectors stabilize. When Cotton suffers, the shock ripples across the entire system.

#### 2.1 Cotton’s Role in the National Economy

Cotton supports one of Pakistan’s most significant economic ecosystems. It impacts:

* The textile and apparel industry
* Ginning and oil extraction units
* Raw cotton exports
* Cottonseed cake and by-products for livestock
* Rural labour and transport services

A strong cotton season boosts foreign exchange earnings and keeps textile mills globally competitive.

#### 2.2 Cotton’s Importance for Farmers and Rural Livelihoods

For farmers, Cotton is still a cornerstone cash crop because:

* It offers higher gross margins than many cereals
* It fits nicely into mixed cropping systems
* It provides multiple income points: seed cotton, seed, oil, and cake
* It supports seasonal employment for rural communities

Cotton harvest and ginning create jobs across farming, transport, and processing hubs.

#### 2.3 Scientific and Research Significance

Agricultural scientists focus intensely on Cotton because it responds quickly to:

* Genetic improvements
* Pest resistance strategies
* Nutrient optimizations
* Climate-smart irrigation scheduling

Cotton is one of the best crops for on-farm trials because management changes show clearly measurable results in boll number, seed weight, and fibre quality.

#### 2.4 Industrial Importance and Value Chain Stability

Cotton drives Pakistan’s textile sector, which accounts for a large share of export earnings. Its value chain includes:

* Ginning
* Spinning
* Weaving and knitting
* Garment manufacturing
* Oil and feed production

Every stage depends on a stable supply of raw cotton.

#### 2.5 Why Cotton Still Matters Despite Climate Challenges

Cotton remains critical because:

* New heat-tolerant and pest-tolerant varieties are emerging
* Improved IPM reduces pesticide loads
* Better irrigation methods stabilize flowering
* Fibre quality innovations keep mills competitive

Even with climate pressure, Cotton’s long-term potential remains strong if modern field management is applied.

## 3. Cotton Growing Zones & Climate

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** is shaped directly by the climate. You can select the best variety, manage nutrition, and control pests, but if the crop is grown outside its climatic comfort zone or sown at the wrong moment, yield potential collapses. Cotton responds sharply to temperature, humidity, and day length—and each production zone in Pakistan has its own climate challenges. Understanding these zones isn’t optional; it’s the foundation of thoughtful planning.

#### 3.1 Punjab Cotton Belt

Punjab produces the majority of the country’s Cotton, but performance varies by district.

##### Key districts:

* Bahawalpur
* Bahawalnagar
* Rahim Yar Khan
* Vehari
* Lodhran
* Multan
* Khanewal
* Muzaffargarh

##### Climate traits:

* Hot summers with long day length
* Mild to warm temperatures during flowering
* Strong sunlight supporting boll setting
* Heatwaves are possible during squaring if sowing is delayed

### **Implications:**

Farmers who adjust sowing time to dodge early heat stress achieve better boll retention and fewer fruiting losses.

#### 3.2 Sindh Cotton Zone

Coastal humidity and warm night temperatures influence Sindh’s cotton belt.

##### Key districts:

* Sanghar
* Mirpur Khas
* Hyderabad
* Tando Allahyar
* Badin
* Thatta
* Nawabshah

##### Climate traits:

* Earlier warming compared to Punjab
* Higher humidity increases whitefly risk
* Longer growing window
* Vulnerability to late-season pest pressure

### **Implications:**

Sindh farmers benefit from early sowing to escape peak pest seasons and manage heat during flowering.

#### 3.3 Emerging Cotton Areas in KP and Balochistan

While these regions are not major producers, cotton cultivation is expanding.

##### KP (limited pockets):

* D.I. Khan
* Tank
* Lakki Marwat

Climate favours Cotton where irrigation is reliable, and sowing is timely.

##### Balochistan:

* Nasirabad division
* Jaffarabad
* Sohbatpur

These regions need heat-tolerant varieties and efficient irrigation, as high evapotranspiration can affect early growth.

#### 3.4 Climate Requirements for Strong Cotton Performance

Cotton thrives when these conditions are met:

##### Ideal temperature ranges:

* **Germination:** 20–30°C
* **Vegetative growth:** 25–35°C
* **Flowering:** 28–34°C (beyond 38°C causes severe shedding)
* **Boll development:** 25–32°C

##### Humidity & pest interaction:

* High humidity increases whitefly and leaf spot risk
* Hot-dry conditions encourage red cotton bug and thrips

##### Rainfall timing:

* Early monsoon rains benefit the crop
* Heavy rains during flowering cause shedding
* Moisture at the boll opening increases rot and contamination

#### 3.5 Why Climate Matching Matters

A cotton field succeeds or fails based on:

* Sowing at the correct climate window
* Matching variety with local heat tolerance
* Managing irrigation timing to avoid stress
* Monitoring pests based on humidity patterns

When climate alignment is correct, **Cotton Production in Pakistan** becomes far more predictable and less risky.

## 4. Best Cotton Varieties in Pakistan

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** depends heavily on selecting varieties that match heat, pest pressure, soil type, and regional climate. Cotton is unforgiving when the genetics don’t fit the environment — even with perfect management, a mismatched variety can’t be saved. The growers who consistently outperform district averages are the ones who select varieties based on *performance traits*, not just seed availability.

#### 4.1 High-Performing Cotton Varieties for Punjab

Punjab needs varieties that withstand high daytime temperatures while maintaining boll retention.

##### Widely adopted performers:

* **NIAB-777** – stable Boll set, good fibre length
* **FH-142** – strong early vigour, suited for central Punjab
* **IUB-2013** – heat-tolerant, long fruiting window

##### Why these work:

* Good performance under variable irrigation

##### Strong options:

* **Sindh-1** – stable yield across seasons
* **Kiran-95** – reliable in coastal belts

##### Why these work:

* Better tolerance to whitefly and leaf curl pressure

* Fit Sindh’s more extended heat period

#### 4.3 Heat-Tolerant and Stress-Resistant Varieties

These varieties perform better under climate extremes:

* **NIAB-Kiran**
* **But varieties with heat-tolerant traits**
* **Lines tested under hot, arid conditions in Bahawalpur & Rahim Yar Khan**

**These regions benefit from varieties with:**

* Strong early vigour

#### 4.5 What Farmers and Scientists Should Consider When Selecting a Variety

##### Key selection criteria:

* Heat tolerance

* Fibre length (28–30 mm preferred)

Growers who consider these traits rather than following neighbours’ choices consistently achieve more stable fibre quality and better seed cotton yields.

## 5. Soil Requirements for Cotton

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** depends heavily on soil quality because Cotton is a deep-rooted, long-duration crop that reacts sharply to poor structure, compaction, and nutrient imbalance. Cotton roots travel deep and wide when soil conditions are right, but they stall immediately when the soil is tight, poorly drained, or low in organic matter. Strong soils lead to stronger boll retention, better nutrient uptake, and superior fibre quality — weak soils force Cotton into Survival Mode.

#### 5.1 Ideal Soil Types for Cotton

Cotton thrives best in soils that balance drainage with moisture-holding capacity.

##### Preferred soils:

* Sandy loam
* Loam
* Silty loam
* Clay loam (well-drained)

##### Cotton struggles in:

* Heavy clay soils with poor drainage
* Hardpan layers restricting root penetration
* Very sandy soils with low nutrient retention
* Salt-affected soils

Punjab’s sandy-loam regions perform well due to natural aeration, while Sindh’s heavier soils need better land preparation to avoid waterlogging early in the season.

##### 5.2 Soil Structure and Why It Matters

Cotton demands:

* Deep rooting zone (up to 4–5 feet)
* Loose, aerated soil for root expansion
* Consistent moisture availability
* Zero standing water

Poor soil structure leads to:

* Stunted early growth
* Weak root anchorage
* Reduced boll load
* Higher susceptibility to wilt and root rots

When the structure is proper, **Cotton Production in Pakistan** becomes far easier because the crop naturally stabilizes its growth.

#### 5.3 Soil Fertility Requirements

##### Essential fertility traits:

* Medium to high organic matter
* Balanced NPK
* Good micronutrient levels
* Adequate sulphur for boll development
* Healthy soil microbial activity

##### Micronutrients essential for Cotton:

* **Zinc** – early vigour
* **Boron** – flower retention and boll set
* **Iron** – leaf colour and enzyme activity
* **Manganese** – photosynthesis stability

Ignoring boron and zinc is one of the biggest silent yield killers in Cotton.

#### 5.4 Soil pH Requirements

Cotton performs best in:

* **pH 6.0–7.5**

Cotton tolerates slightly alkaline soils, but at pH above 8.2:

* Phosphorus becomes unavailable
* Zinc deficiency becomes more severe
* Boron availability decreases
* Root development slows

Managing pH with gypsum, organic matter, and balanced fertilization helps stabilize performance.

#### 5.5 Soil Preparation Practices That Boost Yield

##### Effective preparation includes:

* Deep ploughing once before the season
* Laser levelling for irrigation efficiency
* Creating a fine, firm seedbed
* Breaking hardpan layers if present
* Adding organic matter before sowing
* Preparing ridges or beds in heavy soils

##### Bed planting advantages:

* Improved drainage
* Lower risk of waterlogging
* Easier weed control
* Better ball retention in humid zones

##### Flat sowing advantages:

* Suitable for sandy-loam regions
* Better for fields with strong natural drainage

#### 5.6 Why Soil Quality Directly Controls Yield

When the soil is optimized:

* Roots explore deeper layers
* Plants handle heat and drought better
* Fertilizer efficiency increases
* Boll retention improves
* Fibre quality becomes more consistent
* Pest pressure decreases due to stronger plant vigour

Healthy soil allows **Cotton Production in Pakistan** to reach its true genetic potential without excessive input costs.

## 6. Cotton Sowing Time in Pakistan

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** succeeds or fails based mainly on sowing time. No decision has a greater impact on yield than the timing of planting. Cotton is susceptible to heat during squaring and flowering, and it is equally affected by late-season pest buildup. The growers who consistently achieve strong yields are the ones who plant within the ideal climatic window—neither too early nor too late.

Getting sowing time right protects the crop from:

* Early heat stress
* Late-season pest pressure
* Heavy monsoon damage
* Poor ball retention
* Reduced fibre quality

Cotton rewards disciplined timing more than any other cultural practice.

#### **6.1 Ideal Co**t**ton Sowing Window for Punjab**

Punjab is the backbone of **Cotton Production in Pakistan**, and sowing windows here determine national output.

##### Recommended sowing time:

* **Early window:** 1 April – 15 April
* **Optimal window:** 15 April – 31 May
* **Last acceptable window:** Till 10 June (with risk)

##### Why these dates matter:

* Early-to-mid April sowing avoids heat stress during flowering
* May sowing aligns fruiting with stable weather
* Late sowing increases pink bollworm, whitefly, and shedding losses

Fields sown after mid-June consistently lose yield due to poor boll retention and pest outbreaks.

#### 6.2 Ideal Cotton Sowing Window for Sindh

Sindh’s warmer climate pushes the sowing schedule earlier.

##### Recommended sowing time:

* **Optimal:** 25 March – 30 April
* **Extended:** Till 15 May under irrigated conditions

##### Why Sindh must sow earlier:

* Avoids intense heat during squaring
* Reduces whitefly buildup
* Prevents monsoon-related shedding
* Ensures better boll maturity before humidity spikes

Sindh farmers who sow in March often escape heavy pest pressure later.

#### 6.3 Sowing Guidelines for KP and Balochistan

##### KP:

* **Recommended:** 1 April – 20 May
* Works well in irrigated pockets

##### **Balochistan (Na**s**irabad belt):**

* **Recommended:** 10 April – 25 May

In both regions, heat tolerance is key, and sowing should align with irrigation availability.

#### 6.4 Why Early Sowing Improves Boll Retention

Early-sown cotton benefits from:

* Stable temperatures during fruiting
* Lower pest pressure
* Better root development
* Enhanced nutrient uptake
* Stronger plant vigour
* Higher boll size and boll weight

Early sowing prevents the crop from flowering during the hottest weeks of the season.

#### **6.5 Risks of Late Sowing**

Late sowing exposes Cotton to:

* Heat waves during squaring
* Higher whitefly population
* Increased pink bollworm infestation
* Weak fruiting structures
* Lower seed cotton yield
* Poor fibre maturity and micronaire issues

Late-sown Cotton often produces high vegetation but low boll load—an unprofitable combination.

#### 6.6 Sowing Method and Depth

##### Recommended depth:

* **1–1.5 inches** (2.5–3.5 cm)

##### Common methods:

* Dibbling
* Drill sowing
* Bed planting
* Ridge sowing (in heavy soils)

##### Key requirements:

* Adequate moisture at sowing
* Fine seedbed
* Uniform seed spacing
* Clean the field to prevent early weed stress

Uniform sowing protects the stand and ensures synchronized fruiting later.

#### 6.7 Why Sowing Time is the Strongest Yield Decider

Because Cotton reacts directly to climate:

* Sowing early aligns flowering with ideal temperatures
* Pest cycles can be predicted and avoided
* Irrigation becomes more effective
* Plants retain more flowers and squares
* Boll opening improves fibre quality

Good timing turns **Cotton Production in Pakistan** from a risky crop into a manageable, profitable one.

## 7. Cotton Plant Population & Spacing

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** delivers its best results when plant population is optimized—not too dense, not too sparse. Cotton is susceptible to overcrowding because it needs light penetration, airflow, and room for fruiting branches. At the same time, wide gaps reduce boll load and cause uneven maturity. The most productive fields across Punjab and Sindh maintain a uniform stand with zero missing plants and balanced spacing.

Proper plant population improves:

* Boll retention
* Fruit distribution
* Pest resistance
* Nutrient uptake efficiency
* Irrigation efficiency
* Fibre uniformity

A disciplined stand is one of the strongest predictors of a high-yield cotton crop.

#### 7.1 Optimal Plant Population for Pakistan Conditions

##### Recommended plant population:

* **15,000–17,500 plants per acre** (ideal range)
* **Up to 20,000 plants per acre** in high-vigour varieties
* **13,000–15,000 plants per acre** in dry or sandy soils

Farmers who maintain this density consistently outperform fields with random or uneven stands.

#### 7.2 Recommended Row and Plant Spacing

##### Standard spacing (Punjab & Sindh):

* **Row spacing:** 2.5 feet (75 cm)
* **Plant spacing:** 9–12 inches (22–30 cm)

##### For heat-tolerant and vigorous varieties:

* **Row spacing:** 2.75–3 feet (85–90 cm)
* **Plant spacing:** 8–10 inches (20–25 cm)

##### For areas with high pest pressure:

* Slightly wider spacing improves airflow and reduces whitefly/leaf spot conditions.

#### **7.3 Why the** **Uniform Plant Stand Matters**

Cotton behaves poorly in uneven fields. Missing plants cause:

* Empty patches
* Larger weeds
* Poor canopy structure
* Irregular fruiting
* Increased bollworm attack
* Uneven maturity

A uniform stand produces synchronized flowering and boll setting.

#### 7.4 Planting Methods to Achieve Uniform Population

##### 1. Drill Sowing (Best for large acreage)

* Ensures even seed distribution
* Gives uniform depth
* Allows mechanical weeding

##### 2. Dibbling (Good for precision)

* Better control over seed placement
* Useful for small and medium farms

##### 3. Bed Planting

* Better drainage
* Reduces root diseases in humid zones
* Supports mechanical operations

##### 4. Ridge Planting

* Useful in heavy soils
* Protects plant roots from waterlogging

#### 7.5 Thinning and Gap Filling

Even when sowing is perfect, some seeds fail. Fixing gaps early improves final yield.

##### Thinning:

* Conduct 12–18 days after germination
* Remove weak or double plants
* Maintain recommended spacing

##### Gap filling:

* Fill missing spots within 10 days of germination
* Use pre-germinated seed if possible
* Critical for maintaining uniformity

Skipping thinning and gap filling can cut yield by 8–12%.

#### **7.6 Population Effects on Boll Formation and Fibre Quality**

Correct population leads to:

* More fruiting branches
* Better boll distribution up the plant
* Lower shedding
* Improved boll weight
* Better fibre length and strength

Poor population leads to high vegetative growth but low boll load—a common problem in late-sown Cotton.

#### 7.7 Why Population Management Is a High-Impact Practice

Simply maintaining the correct number of plants per acre consistently adds:

* Higher boll count
* Better fruiting uniformity
* Reduced pest pressure
* More stable yields
* Improved fibre uniformity for mills

Population management is one of the cheapest yet most effective tools in Cotton Production

## 8. Cotton Fertilizer Schedule & Nutrition

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** responds more strongly to balanced nutrition than almost any other field crop. Cotton is a heavy nutrient extractor, and it punishes imbalance. Too much nitrogen causes rank growth, delayed maturity, and increased pest pressure. Too little phosphorus weakens roots and reduces Boll set. Missing boron or zinc quietly destroys flowering without farmers even noticing. When nutrition is balanced, the crop produces uniform fruiting, stronger boll retention, and superior fibre quality.

The difference between an average and an excellent cotton field is almost always **nutrient management**, not seed brand or pesticide choice.

#### 8.1 Baseline Fertilizer Recommendation (Per acre)

These values work well in most regions of Punjab and Sindh and can be adjusted with soil tests.

##### Nitrogen (N): 90–110 kg/acre

* Split into **three applications** for best results
* Heavy nitrogen at the start leads to excessive vegetative growth
* Final dose must not be delayed—delays cause weak boll development

##### Phosphorus (P₂O₅): 30–40 kg/acre

* Apply **100% at sowing**
* Essential for root strength, early vigour, and fruiting branches

##### Potassium (K₂O): 30–50 kg/acre

* Improves boll weight, fibre strength, and drought tolerance
* Important in Sindh’s potassium-deficient soils

##### Sulphur (S): 15–20 kg/acre

* Mandatory for the formation of the Boll and oil synthesis
* Gypsum or ammonium sulphate improves uptake

Fields missing sulphur show **weak fruiting and poor fibre fineness** even when NPK looks correct.

#### 8.2 Micronutrients That Truly Matter in Cotton

Micronutrient deficiencies cause invisible yield losses. Cotton is susceptible to:

##### **1. Boron (B)**

* Critical for flower retention
* Prevents square shedding
* Improves ball weight
* Recommended: **0.5–1.0 kg B/acre**, applied through soil or foliar spray

##### 2. Zinc (Zn)

* Supports early vigour, leaf health, and chlorophyll formation
* Especially important in high pH soils
* Recommended: **5–10 kg Zinc Sulphate/acre**

##### 3. Iron & Manganese

* Needed in calcareous soils
* Improves photosynthesis and stress handling

Correcting micronutrient deficiencies can increase yield by **10–15%** without increasing NPK inputs.

#### 8.3 Fertilizer Splitting Strategy (High-Yield Practice)

##### Application plan:

* **At sowing:** Full P + Full S + 25% N
* **1st irrigation (20–25 days):** 40% N + 50% K
* **2nd irrigation (45–50 days):** Remaining N + Remaining K
* **Early boll set (optional):** Micronutrient foliar spray

Splitting nitrogen prevents rank growth and matches uptake with crop demand.

#### **8.4 Orga**n**ic Matter and Soil Conditioning**

Cotton’s yield potential doubles when the soil contains adequate organic matter.

Adding:

* Farmyard manure (FYM)
* Compost
* Poultry manure
* Press mud
* Green manure crops

These improve:

* Soil structure
* Moisture retention
* Microbial activity
* Nutrient availability
* Root depth

Organic matter also reduces fertilizer waste by improving nutrient retention.

#### **8.5 Com**mon Nutrient Problems in Cotton Fields

##### Nitrogen Excess

* Lush foliage
* Late maturity
* More whitefly and jassid attack

##### Phosphorus Deficiency

* Weak roots
* Poor fruiting branches
* Delayed flowering

##### **Potassium De**f**iciency**

* Small bolls
* Weak fibre
* Poor ball retention

##### Boron Deficiency

* Square and flower shedding
* Hollow bolls
* Weak seed formation

Every deficiency shows up in yield and fiber quality — sometimes more than in visible symptoms.

#### 8.6 Why Balanced Nutrition Improves Fiber Quality

Good nutrition improves:

* Fiber length
* Micronaire value
* Strength
* Uniformity
* Ginning output

Mills reject Cotton with poor micronaire or short staple length. Balanced nutrition protects both yield and fiber grade.

#### 8.7 Why Nutrition Decides the Success of Cotton Production

Balanced fertilizers help **Cotton Production in Pakistan** by:

* Increasing boll load
* Improving boll weight
* Reducing shedding
* Strengthening stress tolerance
* Enhancing fibre quality
* Boosting overall profitability

No single management practice affects Cotton as strongly as correct nutrition.

## 9. Cotton Irrigation Requirements

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** is profoundly shaped by how well farmers manage water. Cotton isn’t a water-loving crop — in fact, it performs best under controlled, well-timed irrigation rather than heavy watering. The crop is susceptible to both drought stress and over-irrigation. Too little water leads to square shedding, poor boll formation, and weak fibre. Too much water leads to root diseases, excessive vegetative growth, and higher whitefly pressure.

The strongest cotton fields are almost always those with **precisely timed irrigation**, not the ones receiving the most water.

#### 9.1 Critical Irrigation Stages in Cotton

Cotton needs water at specific points, not continuously. Irrigate at the wrong time, and yield collapses.

##### Must-serve stages:

* **1st irrigation** – 20–25 days after sowing
* **Square formation** – around 40–45 days
* **Flowering** – the most critical stage
* **Early boll formation** – stabilizes boll size
* **Late boll filling** – maintains boll weight

Missing irrigation during flowering can cut yield by **30–40%**.

#### 9.2 Irrigation Schedule for Punjab

Punjab’s climate allows moderate irrigation, but timing matters more than volume.

##### Recommended plan:

* **4–6 irrigations** for the whole crop cycle under normal conditions
* More in sandy soils
* Less in clay-loam soils with good moisture retention

Punjab farmers who over-irrigate early end up with tall plants and low boll load—a common problem.

#### 9.3 Irrigation Schedule for Sindh

Sindh’s higher temperatures and warm nights require earlier, slightly more frequent watering.

##### Recommended plan:

* **5–7 irrigations** depending on soil type
* Avoid over-irrigation during high humidity — it fuels whitefly expansion
* Focus on stable moisture during flowering and boll formation

Sindh fields perform best when irrigation is light but timely.

#### 9.4 Water Management in KP & Balochistan

##### KP (Irrigated pockets):

* Maintain light, frequent irrigation
* Avoid long dry spells — they cause fruiting delay

##### Balochistan (Nasirabad belt):

* Irrigate early with deeper intervals
* Use ridges to prevent water stagnation

These regions depend heavily on the timing of canal water.

#### 9.5 Signs of Moisture Stress in Cotton

##### Drought stress indicators:

* Leaf cupping
* Square drop
* Slow height increase
* Fewer fruiting branches
* Small bolls

##### Excess moisture indicators:

* Yellowing of lower leaves
* Root rot in heavy soils
* Stunted root system
* High vegetative growth
* More whitefly activity

Most fields suffer more from **over-irrigation** than drought.

#### 9.6 Best Irrigation Methods for Cotton

##### 1. Furrow Irrigation (Recommended)

* Ensures water reaches the root zone
* Reduces stem rot
* More efficient than flood irrigation

##### 2. Bed Planting with Controlled Irrigation

* Prevents waterlogging
* Increases ball set
* Supports better aeration

##### 3. Drip Irrigation (High-efficiency)

* Saves up to 40–50% water
* Boosts boll weight
* Controls moisture precisely
* Improves fertilizer use efficiency

##### 4. Flood Irrigation (Least efficient)

* Should be avoided on heavy soils
* Causes nutrient leaching
* Encourages pests and diseases

#### 9.7 Why Proper Irrigation Improves Fibre Quality

Stable water supply influences:

* Fiber length
* Micronaire
* Boll weight
* Uniformity

Plants under water stress produce shorter, weaker fibre — mills penalize this with lower rates.

#### 9.8 Why Water Management Is a High-Impact Practice

Correct irrigation strengthens **Cotton Production in Pakistan** by:

* Reducing flower and square shedding

* Shielding the crop during heat spells
* Preventing disease outbreaks

Water timing — not water volume — is the fundamental foundation of profitable cotton farming.

## 10. Cotton Pest Management (IPM)

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** faces intense pest pressure because Cotton is a long-duration, nutrient-rich crop that attracts a wide range of insects—no other significant field crop demands such disciplined monitoring. The best growers don’t rely on spraying as a reflex—they follow Integrated Pest Management (IPM), which focuses on prevention, early detection, crop hygiene, and threshold-based decision-making. IPM reduces costs, protects beneficial insects, and prevents the buildup of resistance.

Cotton responds strongly to intelligent pest management, and poorly to blind spraying. When IPM is properly followed, farmers consistently achieve better boll retention, stronger plant vigour, and higher net profit.

#### 10.1 Why IPM Is Critical for Cotton Fields

Cotton suffers rapid yield losses when pests cross damaging levels. IPM helps farmers:

* Prevent early outbreaks
* Avoid unnecessary pesticide use
* Maintain natural predators
* Reduce resistance in pink bollworm and whitefly
* Improve fruiting and boll formation
* Maintain cleaner, healthier fields

IPM is the backbone of successful **Cotton Production in Pakistan**.

#### 10.2 Major IPM Components for Cotton

##### 1. Clean Field Preparation

* Remove previous crop residues
* Deep ploughing to destroy overwintering pests
* Maintain field sanitation all season

##### 2. Early Sowing Synchronization

* Early sowing reduces exposure to peak whitefly season
* Allows the crop to escape severe pink bollworm pressure

##### 3. Resistant Varieties

* Use heat- and pest-tolerant varieties suited to your region
* Avoid mixing seed from unknown sources

##### 4. Crop Monitoring (Weekly Scouting)

Check for:

* Nymphs on leaves
* Pest eggs on tender shoots
* Sooty mould presence
* Leaf curling
* Boll or square damage

##### 5. Threshold-Based Spray Decisions

* Spray only when economic threshold levels (ETLs) are crossed — not randomly.

##### 6. Use of Biological Controls

* Beneficial insects (ladybirds, lacewings, parasitic wasps)
* Neem-based extracts
* Pheromone traps

#### 10.3 Avoiding Common IPM Mistakes

Farmers often make these costly errors:

* Overuse of nitrogen → increases pest attraction
* Excessive early spraying → kills beneficial predators
* Using broad-spectrum insecticides repeatedly
* Spraying without checking ETLs
* Late sowing → highest pest pressure window

Correcting these mistakes often improves yields by **10–20%**.

#### 10.4 Pest Scouting Methods

##### **For whitefly:**

Check the underside of leaves in the morning.

##### For Jassid:

Tap upper leaves gently and observe movement.

##### For thrips:

Inspect new leaves for silvering and curling.

##### For pink bollworm:

Use pheromone traps and dissect fallen squares.

Consistent scouting is the most potent part of IPM.

#### 10.5 Preventive Measures for All Cotton Fields

* Maintain optimum plant spacing
* Avoid waterlogging
* Remove volunteer cotton plants
* Use yellow sticky traps
* Restrict visitors & machinery in infested fields
* Keep bunds clean from weeds

#### 10.6 Why IPM Reduces Cost and Increases Profit

Proper IPM:

* Cuts pesticide cost by 25–40%
* Improves ball retention
* Reduces fruit shedding
* Protects beneficial insects
* Prevents resistance in pest populations

Chemical sprays become *more effective* when used strategically.

#### 10.7 How IPM Supports Long-Term Cotton Health

IPM strengthens the entire ecosystem of **Cotton Production in Pakistan** by:

* Stabilizing field conditions
* Lowering the risk of pest explosions
* Protecting yield even in tough seasons
* Supporting sustainable production
* Reducing contamination in the fibre

A well-managed IPM program is the most reliable way to defend cotton yield without harming fibre quality or soil health.

## 11. Cotton Disease Management

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** is heavily influenced by disease pressure, especially in the humid districts of Sindh and parts of southern Punjab. Cotton diseases don’t just reduce leaf health—they hit boll retention, weaken fibre quality, slow plant vigour, and leave the crop more vulnerable to pests. The biggest challenge is that most diseases spread silently before symptoms become apparent. That’s why the best growers act early, maintain airflow, and follow preventive care rather than reacting too late.

Disease-free Cotton consistently produces more uniform bolls, cleaner fibre, and stronger yields.

#### 11.1 Major Cotton Diseases in Pakistan

##### 1. Cotton Leaf Curl Virus (CLCuV)

The most damaging disease affecting **Cotton Production in Pakistan**.

##### Symptoms:

* Upward curling of leaves
* Thickened veins
* Stunted growth
* Poor fruiting and severe yield loss

##### Management:

* Use CLCuV-tolerant varieties
* Early sowing (before whitefly peak)
* Strict whitefly control
* Remove alternate hosts
* Avoid late nitrogen
* Use recommended seed sources only

#### 2. Leaf Spot Diseases (Alternaria & Cercospora)

##### Symptoms:

* Round to irregular brown spots
* Defoliation in humid weather
* Reduced photosynthesis

##### Management:

* Improve airflow (proper spacing)
* Avoid overhead irrigation during high humidity
* Timely fungicide spray when disease appears
* Balanced nutrition (adequate potassium)

#### 3. Bacterial Blight

##### Symptoms:

* Water-soaked lesions on leaves
* Angular leaf spots
* Stem cankers in severe infections

##### Management:

* Use certified, resistant seed
* Seed treatment
* Avoid mechanical injuries to plants
* Follow rotation with non-host crops

#### 4. Root Rot (Fusarium, Rhizoctonia)

##### Symptoms:

* Sudden wilting
* Brown discolouration in roots
* Poor growth in patches

##### Management:

* Avoid over-irrigation
* Improve soil drainage
* Treat seeds with fungicide
* Add organic matter to strengthen root health

#### **5. B**o**ll** **Rot**

##### Symptoms:

* Wet, mushy bolls
* Discolored lint
* Fungal smell inside bolls

##### Management:

* Avoid dense canopy
* Improve airflow
* Timely fungicide application
* Maintain clean picking at harvest

#### 11.2 Preventive Disease Management Practices

##### Before Sowing:

* Select resistant varieties
* Use certified clean seed
* Treat the seed with fungicide
* Prepare well-drained fields
* Ensure balanced nutrients

##### **During Growth:**

* Maintain proper row spacing
* Keep the field weed-free
* Monitor humidity trends
* Avoid unnecessary nitrogen
* Control whitefly early

##### At Fruiting & Boll Set:

* Improve airflow
* Avoid over-irrigation
* Apply targeted fungicide if necessary

#### 11.3 Climate and Disease Interaction

Disease pressure increases with:

* Monsoon humidity
* Warm nights
* Excess irrigation
* Poor airflow
* Dense canopy structure

Sindh faces higher levels of leaf spot and bacterial blight due to high humidity. Punjab faces more root rot in poorly drained soils.

#### 11.4 Why Preventive Disease Control Saves Yield

Cotton diseases rarely show sudden symptoms—they build slowly and cause continuous damage.

##### A disciplined disease plan helps:

* Maintain leaf area for photosynthesis
* Support the ball formation and filling
* Reduce shedding
* Preserve fibre strength
* Improve uniformity

One unnoticed disease can reduce yield by **15–30%** across the season.

#### 11.5 Why Disease Management Protects Cotton Production in Pakistan

Strong disease management strategies ensure:

* More predictable yields
* Cleaner fields
* Better economic returns
* Stable fibre quality
* Reduced chemical usage
* Lower risk of combined pest–disease outbreaks

Healthy plants tolerate heat, pests, and irrigation stress far more effectively.

## 12. Cotton Weed Control

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** suffers heavily when weeds are not controlled early. Cotton seedlings grow slowly in the first 25–35 days, while most weeds grow fast and aggressively. If weeds take over during this short window, the crop loses nutrients, moisture, light, and space — and that damage cannot be fixed later, no matter how much fertilizer or pesticide you apply. Farmers who keep their fields weed-free in the early stage consistently achieve higher boll load, stronger branching, and more uniform growth.

Weed competition is one of the most underestimated yield killers in Cotton.

#### 12.1 Why Weed Control Matters the Most in Early Cotton Growth

The **critical weed-free period** for Cotton is:

##### 0–40 days after sowing

If weeds are removed after this window, recovery is limited because:

* Early branches remain fewer
* Root growth is weakened
* Flowering becomes uneven
* Boll formation drops
* Pest pressure increases due to dense foliage around the crop

Early competition sets the crop back for the entire season.

#### 12.2 Major Weeds in Cotton Fields of Pakistan

##### Broadleaf weeds:

* Bathu (Chenopodium album)
* Jangli Palak (Rumex dentatus)
* Itsit (Trianthema portulacastrum)
* Krund (Amaranthus viridis)

##### Grassy weeds:

* Dumbi Sitti (Cynodon dactylon)
* Swanki (Echinochloa spp.)
* Jungle Saw (Sorghum halepense)

##### Sedges:

* Purple nut sedge (Cyperus rotundus)

These weeds grow faster than Cotton, especially in warm, moist soils.

#### 12.3 Best Weed Management Practices for Cotton

##### 1. Pre-sowing irrigation (Stale seedbed)

* Irrigate, let weeds germinate, then destroy them before sowing
* Reduces early-season weed pressure by 40–60%

##### 2. Pre-emergence herbicides

Applied right after sowing and before Cotton emerges.

***Effective options include:***

* Pendimethalin
* S-metolachlor
* Oxyfluorfen

These herbicides create a chemical barrier that stops weed emergence.

##### 3. Post-emergence herbicides

Used only when weeds cross the threshold.

Choose narrow-spectrum chemicals to avoid hurting Cotton.

##### 4. Mechanical weeding

* Two hoeings (20 and 35 DAS) in line-sown Cotton
* Improves aeration
* Helps maintain soil tilth
* Lowers humidity around the crop (reduces disease pressure)

##### 5. Mulching

Using crop residues or plastic film in drip systems drastically reduces weed pressure.

#### 12.4 Why Row Planting Makes Weed Control Easier

Fields with drill sowing or row planting benefit from:

* Easier intercultivation
* Faster hoeing
* Better herbicide placement
* More uniform plant stands
* Lower pesticide use due to improved airflow

Broadcast sowing makes weed management almost impossible.

#### 12.5 Weed–Pest Interaction (What Most Farmers Ignore)

Heavy weeds lead to:

* More whitefly and jassid breeding
* Higher humidity → leaf spot diseases
* Lower boll retention
* Poor aeration → root rot
* Delayed maturity

Clean fields produce healthier crops and naturally reduce pest attacks.

#### 12.6 Impact of Good Weed Control on Yield and Fibre Quality

##### Good weed management increases:

* Number of fruiting branches
* Total boll load
* Boll weight
* Fibre length and micronaire
* Plant vigour and uniformity

Research trials show a **15–25% increase** in yield from effective early weed control alone.

#### 12.7 Why Weed Control Strengthens Cotton Production in Pakistan

When early weed pressure is removed:

* Plants establish quickly
* Nutrient efficiency increases
* Irrigation becomes more effective
* Pest control requires fewer sprays
* Flowering and boll setting remain stable

A weed-free field sets the foundation for a high-performing cotton crop.

## 13. Cotton Growth Stages

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** becomes far easier to manage when farmers understand the crop’s growth stages. Cotton is not a crop that responds well to uniform treatment — every stage has its own nutrient needs, irrigation requirements, and vulnerability to diseases and pests. When growers time their operations based on plant physiology rather than habit, they consistently achieve higher boll retention and more stable yield.

Understanding growth stages enables farmers to anticipate problems before they occur.

#### **13.1 Stage 1: Germination and Seedling Emergence (0–15 days)**

##### Key processes:

* The seed absorbs moisture
* Radicle and shoot emerge
* First true leaves develop

##### Management priorities:

* Maintain proper moisture
* Avoid crusting of soil
* Prevent early weed competition
* Ensure uniform plant stand

Weak seedlings often lead to reduced fruiting later in the season.

#### 13.2 Stage 2: Vegetative Growth (15–45 days)

##### What happens:

* Rapid leaf growth
* Development of sympodial branches
* Root expansion

##### Management focus:

* Balanced fertilizer early (avoid too much nitrogen)
* Weed control
* Avoid over-irrigation (prevents rank growth)
* Monitor for jassid and thrips

Strong vegetative growth sets the foundation for good fruiting.

#### 13.3 Stage 3: Square Formation (35–55 days)

This is one of the most critical phases of cotton production **in Pakistan**.

#### Key proce**sses:**

* Flower buds (squares) appear
* Plant prepares for reproductive phase

##### Management priorities:

* Maintain moisture (light irrigation)
* Apply micronutrients (boron, zinc)
* Scout for pests (especially thrips and jassid)
* Avoid stress — stress causes a square drop

Square retention determines the final boll load.

#### 13.4 Stage 4: Flowering (55–85 days)

This stage yields more than any other.

##### What happens:

* Flowers appear daily
* Pollination occurs
* Fruit set begins

##### Management focus:

* Ensure steady moisture (most critical irrigation stage)
* Maintain balanced nutrition
* Control whitefly and leaf spots
* Avoid chemical overuse — it harms pollinators

Heat or moisture stress here causes massive shedding.

#### 13.5 Stage 5: Boll Formation (70–110 days)

##### Key processes:

* Bolls enlarge
* Seeds develop
* Fibre elongation begins

##### Management needs:

* Potassium supply for fibre strength
* Avoid waterlogging
* Protect from bollworms
* Maintain a clean canopy for airflow

A strong boll formation ensures a good ginning outturn.

#### 13.6 Stage 6: Boll Maturation (100–140 days)

##### What happens:

* Fiber thickens
* Boll dries internally
* Lint quality is determined

##### Management focus:

* Final irrigation if necessary
* Keep pest pressure low
* Avoid nutrient excess
* Do not spray harsh chemicals — affects micronaire

Any stress here reduces fibre quality.

#### 13.7 Stage 7: Boll Opening and Harvest (130–170 days)

##### Key points:

* Bolls open naturally
* Lint becomes vulnerable to contamination
* Picking should be done at the proper moisture level

##### Management priorities:

* Pick clean and early
* Avoid picking after rain or dew
* Store cotton in dry conditions
* Protect lint from leaf trash

Clean picking improves price at ginning factories.

#### 13.8 Why Understanding Growth Stages Improves Yield

When farmers work with the plant’s rhythm:

* Irrigation becomes more efficient
* Nutrients match the plant’s demand
* Pests are controlled before economic damage
* Square and ball retention improve
* Fibre quality stays high

Growth-stage-based management is one of the strongest tools in **Cotton Production in Pakistan**.

## 14. Cotton Harvesting & Fibre Quality

**Cotton Production in Pakistan** reaches its actual value only when harvesting is done correctly. Many farmers grow a strong crop but lose money at the final stage through poor picking, contamination, or delayed harvest. Cotton is unique because fibre quality depends not only on genetics and season-long management, but also on how carefully the crop is harvested and handled.

Ginners and textile buyers pay higher prices for clean, well-graded fibre. Poor picking practices can downgrade Cotton even if the crop was excellent in the field.

#### 14.1 Ideal Time for Cotton Harvesting

Harvesting should begin when **60–70% of bolls are fully open**.

##### Signs the crop is ready:

* Bolls are fluffy and white
* Lint separates easily from the lock
* Internal moisture is low
* The weather is dry and stable

Harvesting too early results in immature fibre; harvesting too late risks contamination and boll rot.

#### 14.2 Best Harvesting Practices for Pakistan Conditions

##### 1. Pick early in the day

* After the dew dries
* Before humidity rises
* Avoids moisture-related contamination

##### 2. Pick only fully opened bolls

* Immature bolls reduce fibre grade
* Mixed picking lowers ginning outturn

##### 3. Multiple pickings are necessary

* 2 to 3 pickings recommended
* Ensures clean, high-quality fibre

### 4. Do not pull leaves or trash with the lint

* Leaf trash increases cleaning costs
* Lower quality fibre gets a lower price

##### 5. Use clean cloth bags, not plastic

* Plastic fragments contaminate lint
* Mills reject contaminated batches

#### 14.3 Post-Harvest Handling to Protect Fibre Quality

Proper lint handling after picking affects both market price and ginning results.

##### Key steps:

* Keep Cotton dry
* Store in airy, clean places
* Avoid mixing early and late pickings
* Prevent contact with soil and dirt
* Do not compress Cotton too early

Moist, compressed lint grows mould and loses colour grade.

#### 14.4 Factors That Determine Fibre Quality

Fibre quality depends on:

##### Physical traits:

* Fiber length
* Strength
* Micronaire
* Uniformity index
* Colour and brightness

##### Field-level influences:

* Balanced nutrition (especially potassium)
* Proper irrigation
* Low pest load
* Controlled canopy structure
* Clean picking practices

Even a small mistake at harvest can significantly reduce the market price.

#### 14.5 How to Improve Fibre Grade Through Better Practices

##### Practical ways to upgrade fibre:

* Maintain potassium supply during boll formation
* Prevent whitefly honeydew contamination
* Avoid late-season pesticide residues
* Ensure clean harvesting
* Store Cotton away from dust and moisture
* Pick in shallow baskets to avoid crushing lint

These steps increase the likelihood of achieving Premium grades demanded by spinning mills.

#### 14.6 Common Mistakes That Reduce Cotton Quality in Pakistan

* Picking half-opened bolls
* Mixing dry and wet Cotton
* Using plastic bags
* Over-irrigation before harvesting
* Dirty picking practices
* Leaving harvested Cotton on the soil
* Delayed storage during humid days

These mistakes collectively reduce ginning outturn (GOT%) and increase trash content.

#### 14.7 Why Harvesting and Fibre Quality Matter

High-quality fibre ensures:

* Better market rates
* Higher ginning returns
* Strong demand from textile mills
* Premium price for clean lint
* Improved profitability for growers

When harvesting is done correctly, **Cotton Production in Pakistan** becomes more valuable at every stage of the supply chain.

A well-managed cotton crop naturally produces more fruiting branches, better boll load, and stronger fibre quality.

Here’s the straightforward truth: the highest-yielding cotton fields are not the ones using the most inputs — they’re the ones using inputs at the right time.

The right genetics instantly raise yield by 10–20%.

##### Look for varieties with:

* Heat tolerance
* CLCuV tolerance
* Strong ball retention
* High fibre strength
* Good performance in your region
* Proven results in farmers’ fields

Match variety to your local climate—not to market trends.

#### 15.2 Sow Cotton at the Correct Time

Yield potential drops sharply after the ideal window.

##### To improve yield:

* Punjab: sow between **1 April and 31 May**
* Sindh: sow between **25 March and 30 April**

Early sowing reduces pest pressure and improves boll maturity.

#### 15.3 Maintain Correct Plant Population

Uniform stands produce a more stable yield.

##### Target:

* **15,000–17,500 plants/acre**

##### Avoid:

* Missing plants
* Overcrowding
* Uneven spacing

Correct plant population increases boll count and boll weight.

#### 15.4 Apply Balanced Fertilization

Nutrition must match crop demand.

#### High-yield nutrient principles:

* Apply full phosphorus and sulphur at sowing
* Split nitrogen into three doses
* Supply potassium during boll formation
* Apply boron and zinc at critical stages
* Maintain soil organic matter

Balanced nutrition improves fibre length and boll retention.

#### 15.5 Irrigate Wisely — Timing Matters More Than Volume

Cotton doesn’t need heavy irrigation; it needs well-timed irrigation.

##### Golden irrigation rules:

* Avoid over-irrigation early
* Irrigate at square formation
* Maintain moisture at flowering
* Support ball filling
* Prevent waterlogging

Irrigation mistakes constitute a significant cause of shedding.

#### 15.6 Follow Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

IPM protects yield and reduces pesticide costs.

##### Key IPM tools:

* Weekly scouting
* Threshold-based spraying
* Avoid harsh early sprays
* Use pheromone traps
* Maintain clean field boundaries

Pests are easier to control when identified early.

#### 15.7 Prevent & Control Diseases Before They Spread

Disease-free Cotton retains more fruiting bodies.

##### Focus on:

* Whitefly suppression (to prevent CLCuV)
* Adequate airflow
* Preventing leaf spots in high humidity
* Avoiding waterlogging
* Using seed treatment

Healthy plants tolerate stress better.

#### 15.8 Weed Control During the Critical Period

Weeds must be removed during the first 40 days.

##### Essential actions:

* Two hoeings
* Pre-emergence herbicide
* Post-emergence if needed
* Clean rows for airflow

Cleaning early fields ensures vigorous fruiting.

The most successful growers time their actions based on crop physiology — not the calendar.

##### Management aligned with stages:

* Nutrition before flowering
* Irrigation during flowering
* Protection during ball formation
* Clean picking during boll opening

Stage-based decisions improve uniformity and fibre grade.
